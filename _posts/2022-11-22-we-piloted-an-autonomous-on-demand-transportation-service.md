The Autonomous Driving Lab conducted a pilot project deploying a self-driving vehicle as part of Tartu's on-demand transportation initiative. The team operated their autonomous car alongside conventional vans over a two-week period.

## Context and Motivation

While the 20th century centered on personal automobile ownership, this model proves unsustainable for 21st-century mobility. Public transportation offers significantly greater resource efficiency than individual vehicles. However, the Tartu region struggled with adoption, with only 9% of local commuters using public transit.

Outside urban centers, traditional bus services face challenges including sparse populations, inconvenient schedules, and empty runs. On-demand transportation addresses these issues by dispatching vehicles based on passenger requests rather than fixed routes.

## Project Overview

The lab recognized that autonomous vehicles operate more effectively outside cities, where pedestrians and unpredictable urban traffic complicate operations. By combining autonomous technology with on-demand services, they could tackle a complex problem through simpler subtasks.

The partnership involved five stakeholders: the Autonomous Driving Lab (vehicle and operators), Modern Mobility (platform integration), Traffest (traffic signal access to 30 lights), Bercman Technologies (smart intersection equipment), and Tartu city government.

## Results

During operation, the team serviced 33 passengers across 21 rides, traveling 331 kilometers. They achieved their target of 90% autonomous operation. The safety driver intervened 252 times, with approximately 55 disengagements occurring as precautionary measures during passenger boarding and exiting. Additional interventions involved pedestrian crossings (38), swerving (23), rerouting (21), and localization issues (14).

The team concluded that autonomous on-demand transportation represents a realistic and impactful pathway toward sustainable mobility solutions.
