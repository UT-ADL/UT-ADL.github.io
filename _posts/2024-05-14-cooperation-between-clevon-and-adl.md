The University of Tartu Institute of Computer Science and Clevon AS have established a three-year partnership agreement to implement a teleoperation system for the Autonomous Driving Lab's test vehicle. During a technology demonstration, attendees remotely operated the vehicle from nearly three kilometers away, with the car stationed in the Estonian National Museum's parking lot.

Tambet Matiisen, technology manager at the Autonomous Driving Lab, emphasized the significance of remote control capabilities. He noted that "Remote control is an important technical solution in today's autonomous vehicles, allowing the vehicle to seek assistance from a human in unexpected traffic situations, such as roadworks or traffic jams." This technology enables self-driving cars to operate beyond pre-mapped areas and deliver passengers to specific locations.

Clevon values the academic partnership highly. CEO Sander Sebastian Agur stated that "Collaboration provides us with valuable feedback to further develop our platform and create new solutions that meet both current and future transportation needs." The company aims to support research initiatives related to autonomous systems and teleoperations.

Matiisen outlined several research directions for this collaboration, including studying remote operator situational awareness, evaluating cybersecurity vulnerabilities, and exploring alternative control methods like bird's-eye view displays and virtual reality interfaces for remote operators.

Both organizations anticipate the three-year agreement will generate significant advancements in autonomous vehicle development.
