The University of Tartu Autonomous Driving Lab participated in the FOSS4G conference during early July. Tambet Matiisen delivered a presentation titled "Tartu: Pioneering the Future of Self-Driving Technology with Open Source and Open Data."

## Overview

Autonomous vehicles have the potential to improve transportation safety and affordability. While driverless taxis operate in San Francisco, widespread global expansion encounters substantial barriers. Tartu, Estonia serves as a leading European testing environment for autonomous vehicles, utilizing open-source solutions and publicly available data to address regulatory and technical challenges.

## Why Tartu?

Estonia's varied climate patterns, flexible regulatory framework, and strong network of autonomous technology enterprises — including Starship, AuveTech, Clevon, and Milrem Robotics — position Tartu as an ideal experimental location. The University of Tartu's Autonomous Driving Lab drives this initiative.

## Key Vision Components

The lab's strategic objectives for Tartu include:

- Dedicated autonomous vehicle testing regions with both restricted zones and public street areas
- High-definition mapping with spatial point cloud data and lane-level road information
- Digital simulation environment enabling pre-deployment testing
- Connected traffic signals supporting autonomous navigation safety

## Technical Tools

The project leverages open-source platforms including QGIS, Shapely, Blender, and CARLA simulator, combined with resources from the Estonian Land Board and Tartu municipality, achieving decimeter-precision accuracy.

## Exhibition

During the conference, the Autonomous Driving Lab showcased an interactive exhibit allowing participants to experience driving within Tartu's digital simulation environment.
